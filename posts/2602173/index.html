LCD Introduction

LCD Overview

LCD stands for Liquid Crystal Display.

Liquid crystal is a special substance between solid and liquid. Under the action of an electric field, the arrangement of liquid crystal molecules changes, thereby affecting its optical properties.

LCD is also one of the commonly used peripherals. Through LCD, images and interfaces can be displayed, and with touch and mouse, human-computer interaction can be achieved. For example, common computer screens and mobile phone screens in daily life basically have the presence of LCD.

Before the popularization of LCD monitors, CRT monitors were the mainstream display devices, which are commonly known as “big head” monitors.

This type of monitor is rarely seen today. The full name of a CRT monitor is “Cathode Ray Tube”, which uses Cathode Ray Tube technology to achieve image display.

A CRT monitor is mainly composed of five key parts:

  1. Electron Gun used to emit electron beams
  2. Deflection Coils used to control the direction of the electron beam
  3. Shadow Mask used to precisely focus the electron beam
  4. Graphite electrode providing high-voltage support
  5. As well as the phosphor layer coated on the inner wall of the screen and the glass envelope.

These components work together to convert electronic signals into visible images.

In addition to LCDs, more advanced OLED screens have emerged today. For example, many current flagship phones use OLED screens.

The full name of OLED is “Organic Light-Emitting Diode”, and its display principle is different from traditional LCDs:

OLED does not require a backlight; instead, it uses a very thin organic material coating and a glass substrate (or flexible substrate). When an electric current passes through, these organic materials emit light on their own.

LCD Panel

LCD can be subdivided into TFT, TN, IPS, VA, and SLCD, which represent different liquid crystal panel technologies. Each of these technologies has unique characteristics and application scenarios. The specific description of each technology is as follows:

Technology NameFull NameDescription
TFTThin Film TransistorTFT displays are the mainstream display devices on various laptops and desktop computers. Each liquid crystal pixel is driven by a thin-film transistor integrated behind the pixel, making it an active-matrix liquid crystal display device. TFT displays have the advantages of high response, high brightness, and high contrast, with display effects close to CRT monitors. They are among the high-end products in LCD color displays.
TNTwisted NematicTN panels are entry-level liquid crystal panels that are widely used in mid-to-low-end LCD monitors due to their low production cost. Although their performance is relatively basic, their low cost makes them one of the mainstream choices in the market.
IPSIn-Plane SwitchingIPS technology was developed by Hitachi in 1996 and is a wide-viewing-angle liquid crystal display technology. Compared to TN screens, IPS can effectively improve the color shift issues that occur at poor viewing angles, and is widely used in the manufacturing of LCD TVs and tablet computers, providing superior display effects.
VAVertical AlignmentVA panels are a liquid crystal panel technology that compromises the advantages of TN and IPS, offering high contrast and good color performance at a moderate price. Their disadvantage is slower response time, and they are typically used in mid-to-high-end monitors and scenarios with certain image quality requirements, such as home theater monitors.
SLCDSplice Liquid Crystal DisplaySLCD is a high-end derivative of LCD, utilizing industrial-grade liquid crystal panels with a lifespan of up to 60,000 hours. It can be used both as a standalone monitor and spliced into extra-large screens, serving as a complete splicing display unit suitable for high-end display requirement scenarios.

IPS, VA, and TN panels each have their own advantages and disadvantages.

  • In terms of color and color accuracy performance, IPS is the best, followed by VA, and finally TN;
  • In terms of response time, TN panels are the fastest, followed by IPS, and VA is the slowest;
  • In terms of viewing angles, IPS performs the best, followed by VA, and TN is the worst;
  • For contrast, VA panels are the strongest, IPS is in the middle, and TN is the weakest.

Overall, different panel types have their own characteristics in various indicators, and suitable panels can be chosen based on needs.

Pixel

Using the control principles of liquid crystals, a display structure can be designed that adjusts the output intensity of the three colors: red, green, and blue. Combining these three display structures into a single display unit, and by precisely controlling the brightness of each color, the unit can be made to display different colors. Such a display unit is called a ‘pixel’.

Therefore, a pixel can be analogized as a small RGB light, and since RGB are the three primary colors of light, they can be mixed to produce any color.

Resolution

If a screen has a resolution of 1920x1080, then there are a total of 1920x1080=2,073,600 pixels. So resolution is the number of pixels that make up the screen.

Generally speaking, the higher the resolution, the better the display effect, but resolution is not the only standard; screen brightness, color accuracy, and viewing angles are also important parameters that affect the display effect of a screen.

For example, in the image above, the horizontal axis is X and the vertical axis is Y. If it represents a 1920x1080 resolution screen, there are 1920 pixels in the X-axis direction and 1080 pixels in the Y-axis direction. This shows that the higher the resolution, the better the screen display effect. It is an important parameter for measuring the display effect of a screen.

LCD Interface Types

There are many types of LCD interfaces. Common ones in embedded systems include RGB, MIPI, LVDS, and EDP interfaces, while common ones on monitors include DP, HDMI, and VGA interfaces. A brief introduction to each interface is as follows:

Interface NameIntroduction
RGBParallel digital interface, using TTL level to transmit red, green, and blue signals, commonly used in early LCD screens and embedded displays.
MIPIHigh-speed serial digital interface, designed specifically for mobile devices (such as phones/tablets), using differential signal transmission, featuring low power consumption and supporting high resolution.
LVDSLow-voltage differential serial digital interface, with strong anti-interference capability, widely used in mid-to-high-end screens such as notebooks and monitors.
EDPEmbedded DisplayPort interface, based on high-speed serial digital transmission, with a rate far exceeding LVDS, suitable for high-resolution notebooks, tablets, and professional monitors.
VGAAnalog signal interface, transmitting via RGBHV signals, with strong compatibility but limited resolution, gradually being replaced by digital interfaces.
HDMIFully digital audio-visual interface, supporting high resolution and synchronous audio transmission, widely used in TVs, monitors, and consumer electronic devices.

DBI, DPI, and DSI interface categories

DBI, DPI, and DSI interfaces are another classification of screen interfaces by the MIPI Alliance’s working group DWG (Display Working Group), and do not conflict with the RGB, MIPI, LVDS, EDP, etc. interfaces mentioned above.

  • The DBI interface is also called the MCU interface and 8080 interface, and its full name is Display Bus Interface. It can transmit control commands and data through a parallel interface, and refresh the screen by updating data to the GRAM built into the LCD module.

  • DPI stands for Display Pixel Interface, which is a parallel interface. For example, the RGB interface mentioned earlier can be classified as a DPI interface.

  • DSI stands for Display Serial Interface, which is a display serial interface, and is a type of image output interface defined by the MIPI Alliance.

Display Controller

Basic Concepts of Display Controllers

A display controller, also known as LCDC (short for LCD Controller), is primarily responsible for transferring image data from memory to the display device.

With the continuous development of technology, the functions of display controllers have also been enhanced, enabling them to handle some simple image operations, such as scaling, rotation, and image composition.

The role of a display controller in a processor is similar to that of a graphics card in a computer. When a processor integrates a display controller, it is equivalent to having a built-in graphics card module that can directly drive the display to output image signals.

For example, high-performance SoCs like the RK3399, RK3588, and RK3568 all have built-in display controllers, enabling video output without the need for additional hardware.

  3. Mainly applied in fields with basic display requirements, such as industrial control and sensor panels.

Introduction to VOP

In Rockchip’s series of SoCs, the display controller is called VOP (Video Output Processor). Currently, there are two VOP architectures in Rockchip processors: VOP1 and VOP2.

The difference between the two mainly lies in how they support multi-screen display. Typically, a single VOP can only drive one screen for independent display at any given time. If multi-screen display is required, multiple VOPs need to work together.

Connector-mirror Technology

In the RK3568 chip, there are three independent Video Ports (VPs). Therefore, under the most basic conditions, the RK3568 supports three display outputs. If more screens need to be output simultaneously, expansion can be achieved using the connector-mirror technology.

For example, based on its four VOPs, the RK3588, combined with the connector-mirror technology, can achieve application scenarios that output seven display signals simultaneously.

The figure above uses the connector-mirror technology to connect two HDMI/eDP channels to VP0, two DP channels to VP1, and two MIPI DSI channels to VP2. VP3 outputs via BT656 and BT1120, thus achieving the simultaneous output of 7 channels.

Through the above configuration, the system can output up to 7 display signals simultaneously, but these signals are actually achieved through 4 independent display paths. It should be noted thatthe display timing and content output by each group (i.e., the two display interfaces on the same Video Port) are exactly the same。

In this application mode, the maximum resolution of each display pathis limited by the maximum resolution support capability of the corresponding Video Port and the connected display interface. This function can be enabled through DTS (Device Tree Source) configuration. In the DTS file, configuration is completed simply by mounting the two display interfaces under the same Video Port.

Currently, this feature is only supported in the NVR SDK, while Android does not yet support this function.

vop-split technology

The VOP-Split function is a technology similar to the MIPI dual-channel mode, which can divide the output of one Video Port into left and right parts in the horizontal direction, thereby simultaneously driving two display interfaces. In this mode, the display timings of the two display interfaces remain consistent, but they can output screens with independent content respectively.

It should be noted that this function currently only supports hardware platforms based on the VOP2 architecture. By using the VOP-Split function, more efficient display expansion capabilities can be achieved. The application block diagram for expansion using the VOP-Split function is shown below:

After enabling Split mode on VP0, the output of VP0 can simultaneously drive two display interfaces (such as HDMI0/1, eDP0/1, or other display interfaces connected to VP0). The content presented by these two display interfaces is the result of dividing the screen output by VP0 equally into left and right parts in the horizontal direction.

For example, if VP0 outputs at a resolution of 3840x1080, each display interface will display a 1920x1080 image respectively.

It should be noted that in Split mode, the two display interfaces participating in the output on the same VP must maintain the same display timing and frame rate.

Timing

Linux abstracts the time parameters of the LCD, that is, the timing. In the kernel source code:Documentation/fb/framebuffer.rstyou can find the abstract diagram of Linux’s LCD timing, as shown in the figure below

The figure shows a physical device where the “electron gun” draws each pixel point line by line and pixel by pixel in the order from left to right and top to bottom. Until the last pixel point is drawn, the entire frame will not be fully presented. This process is shown in the figure below:

The process of the “electron gun” drawing a frame is completed by scanning line by line in a Z-shape. Each line is drawn from left to right, and after completion, it returns to the starting position of the next line until the entire frame is drawn.

In this process:

  • HSYNC (Horizontal Sync Signal) is used to indicate the start of drawing a new line, so it appears at the far right of each line;
  • VSYNC (Vertical Sync Signal) indicates the start of a new frame, appearing at the bottom of the entire frame.
  • In the abstract diagram, the Hactive and Vactive areas represent the actual image display area, while the surrounding HFP (Horizontal Front Porch), HBP (Horizontal Back Porch), VFP (Vertical Front Porch), and VBP (Vertical Back Porch) together constitute the so-called “black borders”. Their existence is to allow time for signal switching and electron gun retrace.

The specific explanation is as follows:

  1. HFP (Horizontal Front Porch): The time after the current line is drawn, the electron gun is turned off, and before the next HSYNC signal is generated.
  2. HSYNC: Indicates the start of horizontal retrace, the electron gun jumps back to the beginning of the next line.
  3. HBP (Horizontal Back Porch): The waiting time between the end of the HSYNC signal and the electron gun turning back on to start drawing a new line.
  4. VFP (Vertical Front Porch): The time after the entire frame is drawn, the electron gun is turned off, and before the VSYNC signal is generated.
  5. VSYNC: Indicates the start of vertical retrace, the electron gun returns to the top-left corner of the screen to prepare for drawing a new frame.
  6. VBP (Vertical Back Porch): The waiting time between the end of the VSYNC signal and the electron gun turning back on to start drawing a new frame.

Although these “black border” areas do not participate in the actual image display, they are crucial for maintaining image stability and synchronization. The abstract diagram is shown below:

The “electron gun” is relatively common in CRT monitors, but CRT monitors have now been phased out. There is no “electron gun” in LCDs. Instead, chips are used to replace the “electron gun” to control the timing of the LCD.

Synchronization Mode

From the previous LCD timing analysis, it can be seen that the horizontal timing includes HFP (Horizontal Front Porch), HBP (Horizontal Back Porch), and HSYNC (Horizontal Synchronization Signal), while the vertical timing includes VFP (Vertical Front Porch), VBP (Vertical Back Porch), and VSYNC (Vertical Synchronization Signal).

These timing sections do not contain displayable data, so a mechanism is needed between the LCD and the processor to achieve synchronization.

The core function of synchronization is toallow the LCD to recognize the current signal state sent by the processor, for example, when sending non-display timing such as HFP, the LCD will not display it as valid data; or to notify the LCD that starting from a certain point in time, the subsequently transmitted data is valid display data that can be used for screen display.

To achieve this synchronization purpose, there are currently two main methods:

  • DE Mode
  • HV Mode

DE Mode

Using the DE data line to indicate the start and end of valid data, such as the LCDC_DEN signal line.

When the DE signal goes high, it indicates the start of valid data, and when the DE signal goes low, it indicates the end of valid data. Therefore, in DE mode, signal lines such as HSYNC and VSYNC are not required. It is relatively simple to use.

HV Mode

In HV mode, the LCD timing needs to be determined through HSYNC (Horizontal Synchronization signal) and VSYNC (Vertical Synchronization signal), without using the DE (Data Enable) signal line. Synchronization work is entirely dependent on the HSYNC and VSYNC signals.

Currently, most LCD screens support both HV mode and DE mode simultaneously. For example, the following screen is compatible with both modes and selects the specific working mode through its 8th pin:

  • When the 8th pin is at a high level, the screen operates in DE mode;

  • When the 8th pin is at a low level, it switches to HV mode.

This design provides developers with greater flexibility, allowing them to choose the appropriate synchronization method based on actual needs.

LVDS screen porting

Interface introduction

LVDS stands for Low-Voltage Differential Signaling. LVDS has the characteristics of low power consumption, low bit error rate, low crosstalk, low jitter, low radiation, and good signal integrity. It is widely used in display applications, such as laptop screens, medical equipment displays, etc.

In 1994, the ANS/TIA/EIA-644 standard was introduced mainly by National Semiconductor (NS, now TI). In 1996, the IEEE 1596.3 standard was introduced. However, IEEE 1596.3 is rarely used now, and the ANS/TIA/EIA-644 standard is the main one in use. The LVDS interface of the RK3568 processor also adopts this standard. The relevant content in the datasheet is as follows:

LVDS uses differential signals for data transmission, and its basic working principle is as follows:

There is a current source at one end of the transmitter that can provide a current of 3.5ma. There is a 100-ohm resistor at the input end of the receiver. Because the internal resistance of the receiver is very high, the current will return to the transmitter through the 100-ohm resistor, so there will be a voltage of 350mv across the terminal resistor. By changing the direction of the current, the receiver can judge whether the input signal is positive or negative through plus or minus 350mv.

Single-channel can be understood as having only 1 LVDS interface, while dual-channel means having 2 LVDS interfaces. Therefore, when distinguishing between 6-bit and 8-bit, single-channel is taken as an example, because dual-channel only needs to multiply the original by 2.

As the screen resolution increases, the pixel clock also gets higher. However, the pixel clock cannot increase indefinitely, so the pixels to be output are divided into odd pixels and even pixels in order. Odd pixels are transmitted using one LVDS channel, and even pixels are transmitted using one LVDS channel. Therefore, theoretically, dual-channel LVDS supports higher resolutions.

Introduction to MIPI Protocol

lane

The term “lane” is mentioned in the hardware connection block diagram of the MIPI protocol specification. “Lane” can be understood as a channel. For example, 2 lanes can be understood as 2 channels, and each channel has 2 differential data lines. Such as MIPI_DSI_TX1_D0P and MIPI_DSI_TX1_A pair of D0N data pins can be considered as 1 lane, which is 1 channel. In the MIPI chapter, no distinction is made between lane and channel; they mean the same thing.

The block diagram above tells us that we can use 1/2/3/4 lanes and a pair of clock lines for data transmission. For the MIPI interface of the RK3568 core board, 4 lanes are used for data transmission.

The figure above also tells us that in LP (Low-Power) mode, only Lane0 is used for transmission, and bidirectional transmission is possible, while in HS (High-Speed) mode, only unidirectional transmission is possible.

Let’s look at the block diagram of a single lane:

A channel may contain an HS-TX, an HS-RX, or both.

In a single channel, HS-TX and HS-RX are not enabled simultaneously during normal operation, so it is unidirectional transmission for HS (High-Speed) mode.

If the high-speed function in the channel is not enabled, the function should be placed in a high-impedance state.

The Low-Power Contention Detector (LP-CD) function is only used for bidirectional operation. The Low-Power Contention Detector function is enabled when the Low-Power transmitter drives the Low-Power state to detect contention.

Signal level

In LP (Low-Power) mode, only Lane0 is used for transmission, employing single-ended signals with a signal level between 0 and 1.2V.

In HS (High-Speed) mode, differential signals are used for transmission. When P is higher than N, it is defined as 1; when P is lower than N, it is defined as 0. The typical voltage on the differential line is a differential 200mV.

The blue solid line in the figure above is the signal waveform in LP (Low-Power) mode, and the red solid line is the signal waveform in HS (High-Speed) mode.

Channel state

In HS (High-Speed) mode, differential signals are used. When P is higher than N, it is defined as 1, called HS-1; when P is lower than N, it is defined as 0, called HS-0. Therefore, there are two states in HS (High-Speed) mode: HS-1 and HS-0.

In LP (Low-Power) mode, single-ended signals are used, so there are four states in total: 00, 01, 10, and 11, which are called LP-00, LP-01, LP-10, and LP-11 respectively.

Three operating modes

The channel (lane) has:

  • Control mode
  • High-speed mode
  • Escape mode

There are three operating modes: the high-speed mode operates in HS (High-Speed) mode, while the control mode and Escape mode operate in LP (Low-Power) mode.

These three operating modes can be switched among each other. The control mode operates in LP (Low-Power) mode, so there are four states: LP-00, LP-01, LP-10, and LP-11. In the MIPI protocol, different timings composed of the four different states of the control mode are used to represent the upcoming entry or exit of a certain mode. For example, LP11-LP01-LP00 indicates entering High-Speed mode.

According to the MIPI protocol specification, the processes for entering and exiting different modes are as follows:

  1. Each mode must start from the Stop State (LP-11).
  2. High-speed mode and Escape mode cannot be directly switched back and forth; they must transit through the Control mode, namely:
    • High-speed mode ↔ Control Mode ↔ Escape mode.
  3. Process of entering and exiting Escape mode:
    • Request：LP-11→LP-10→LP-00→LP-01→LP-00
    • Exit：LP-10→LP-11
  4. Process of entering and exiting High-Speed mode:
    • Request：LP-11→LP-01→LP-00
    • Exit：EOT →LP-11
  5. Process of entering and exiting Control mode:
    • Request：LP-11→LP-10→LP-00→LP-10→LP-00
    • Exit：LP-00→LP-10→LP-11

Escape mode is a special mode for the data Lane in the LP (Low-Power) state. In this mode, there are functions such as LPDT (Low-Power Data Transmission), ULPS (Ultra-Low Power State), and Trigger. Once entering Escape mode, the transmitter must send an 8-bit command to respond to the requested action. The commands are as follows:

Introduction to MIPI D-PHY

In addition to D-PHY, MIPI also has C-PHY and M-PHY. PHY refers to the physical layer. For example, D-PHY can be used for camera and display peripherals, C-PHY can also be used for camera and display peripherals, and M-PHY can be used for camera and storage peripherals.

The D in D-PHY refers to the Roman numeral 500 (the original design goal was 500 Mbits/s), not Display. C and M represent the Roman numerals 100 and 1000, respectively.

PHY layer

The physical layer is located at the bottom of the entire hierarchical structure, where relevant electrical properties are specified, such as transmission media, electrical characteristics, IO circuits, and synchronization mechanisms.

The physical layer also specifies the mechanisms for Start of Transmission (SoT) and End of Transmission (EoT), as well as other “out-of-band” information that can be passed between the transmitting and receiving physical layers.

The physical layer supports HS (High-Speed) mode and LP (Low-Power) mode, where HS mode can be used to transmit high-speed data, such as screen data. LP mode is generally used for control, such as configuration commands.

Lane Management Layer

The number of data signal pairs may be 1, 2, 3, or 4, depending on the bandwidth requirements of the application. At the transmitter end of the interface, the data stream is distributed onto one or more lanes, functioning as a “distributor”. At the receiver end, the interface collects bytes from the lanes and merges them into a reassembled data stream to recover the original stream sequence, functioning as a “merger”.

A long packet should consist of three parts: a 32-bit Packet Header (PH), an application-specific data payload with a variable number of bytes, and a 16-bit Packet Footer (PF).

HDMI interface introduction

Background of Emergence

In 1998, computer industry giants such as Intel, IBM, HP, Fujitsu, and Compaq jointly launched the DVI (Digital Visual Interface) interface. The DVI interface transmits digital signals based on TMDS (Transition Minimized Differential Signaling) technology. Through an encoding algorithm, 8-bit data (the base color signal of each channel in R, G, B) is encoded into 10-bit data (including horizontal and vertical synchronization information, clock information, data DE, error correction, etc.), and data is transmitted using differential signals.

The DVI interface is widely used in devices such as PCs, DVDs, high-definition televisions (HDTVs), and high-definition projectors. It can support resolutions up to 2K@60Hz and 1080P@120Hz. However, with the popularization of high-definition televisions (HDTVs) in the early 21st century, consumer demand for audio-visual integration surged. TV manufacturers (such as Sony, Panasonic, Hitachi, etc.) believed that DVI could not meet the following requirements:

  1. Televisions and home theater equipment tend to be thinner and lighter, requiring more compact interfaces to achieve device miniaturization, while the DVI interface is bulky, inconvenient to plug and unplug, and difficult to adapt to slim devices.
  2. Digital audio and video need to be transmitted through a single interface to achieve audio-visual integration, while the DVI interface has a single function, only supporting video transmission, requiring additional cables to transmit audio.
  3. Piracy of high-definition content is a serious problem, and the demand for encryption technology is increasing, while the DVI interface does not integrate content encryption technology (such as HDCP).
  4. It is necessary to support higher bandwidth to support emerging resolutions such as 4K and 8K, but the DVI interface has limited bandwidth and struggles to meet the growing demand for resolution.

In 2002, TV manufacturers such as Sony, Panasonic, Hitachi, Toshiba, and Philips jointly established the HDMI consortium, with the goal of designing a next-generation interface to replace DVI, namely the HDMI interface. The full name of the HDMI interface is High Definition Multimedia Interface. In 2003, the HDMI 1.0 standard was officially released, with core features including:

  1. High bandwidth: 5Gbps transmission rate, supporting 1080P@60Hz and uncompressed 8-channel audio.
  2. Miniaturized interface: Volume reduced by about 50% compared to DVI, making plugging and unplugging more convenient.
  3. Audio-visual integration: Single-cable transmission of digital video and audio signals.
  4. Compatibility: Backward compatible with DVI interface via an adapter.
  5. Content protection: Integrated HDCP encryption technology to prevent illegal copying.

Development History of HDMI

HDMI 1.2 (2005): Supports display color depth expansion (8/10/12bit), compatible with SACD audio.

HDMI 1.3 (2006): Bandwidth increased to 10.2Gbps, introduced Deep Color technology, xvYCC wide color gamut.

HDMI 1.4 (2009): Bandwidth 10.2Gbps, new features: supports 4K@30Hz, 1080P@144Hz, 3D video. Added Ethernet Channel (HEC) and Audio Return Channel (ARC).

HDMI 2.0a/b (2013-2016): Bandwidth 18Gbps, supports 4K@60Hz, 1080P@240Hz, introduced static HDR (High Dynamic Range).

HDMI 2.1 (2017): Bandwidth jumped to 48Gbps, core upgrades: Resolution and refresh rate: 8K@60Hz, 4K@120Hz (uncompressed), supports dynamic HDR (Dolby Vision); Gaming optimization: Variable Refresh Rate (VRR), Auto Low Latency Mode (ALLM); Enhanced audio: eARC (Enhanced Audio Return Channel).

Bandwidth calculation

When choosing an HDMI screen, how do you determine if the processor can support a screen of that resolution? This involves HDMI bandwidth calculation. Take the RK3568 processor as an example.

The RK3568 processor supports up to HDMI2.0. In HDMI2.0, the TMDS clock is 600MHz, so the bandwidth under HDMI2.0 is: 600000000x10x3=18Gbps.

The effective bandwidth is 18x(10/8)=14.4Gbps.

Why multiply the effective bandwidth by 10/8? Because in HDMI transmission, 8-bit is converted to 10-bit. Then we calculate the bandwidth required by the HDMI screen. If the bandwidth required by the HDMI screen is less than the processor’s HDMI bandwidth, then the screen can be supported.

HDMI screen bandwidth calculation formula:Htotal x Vtotal x refresh rate x encoding method x bit depth

Where, the value of Htotal is equal to Hsync + Hfront + Hactive + Hback, and the value of Vtotal is equal to Vsync + Vfront + Vactive + Vback. Htotal x Vtotal represents the number of pixels per frame.

The number of pixels per frame multiplied by the monitor’s refresh rate is the amount of pixels the monitor has to process per second, i.e., Htotal x Vtotal x refresh rate. If the encoding method is yuv444, the encoding method value is 1; if the encoding method is yuv422, the encoding method value is 2/3; if the encoding method is yuv420, the encoding method value is 1/2.

Example: A screen with 3920 (Htotal) x 2222 (Vtotal) @60Hz, using yuv444 encoding, and a bit depth of 8 requires a bandwidth of 3920x2222x60x1x8x3, which is approximately 12.543Gbps.

Introduction to TMDS differential signals

Earlier we mentioned that the DVI interface uses TMDS differential signals, and the HDMI interface was developed to replace the DVI interface.

In fact, HDMI originated from DVI interface technology, and the HDMI interface also centers on the TMDS signal transmission technology of the American company Silicon Image. HDMI is pin-compatible with DVI, just using different packaging. Therefore, HDMI and DVI interfaces can be converted to each other via adapters. In principle, HDMI is backward compatible with DVI.

Therefore, this chapter focuses on introducing TMDS differential signals. We will start with the connection framework diagram in the HDMI specification.

As can be seen from the figure, TMDS transmission is divided into two parts: the source (transmitter) and the sink (receiver). The TMDS link includes 3 data transmission channels (Channel 0, 1, 2) and 1 clock signal transmission channel (Clock Channel).

Taking the source on the left as an example, there are a total of 3 data channels, each using parallel transmission with a width of 8 bits. Among them:

  • D[7:0]: Represents the 8-bit actual image data.
  • D[1:0]: In Channel 0, it represents the HSYNC and VSYNC signals; in Channel 1 and Channel 2, it represents the 4 control signals CTL0~CTL3.
  • D[3:0]: Used to represent some additional auxiliary data, such as audio data, etc.

Why does the 8-bit data D[7:0] transmit image data, and then D[1:0] transmits other signals?

In the HDMI transmission overview, the transmission is divided into 3 phases, which are: dark gray for the Video Data Period, dark blue for the Data Island Period, and light gray for the Control Period.

  1. Control Period

The Control Period is used when no video, audio, or auxiliary data needs to be transmitted. There must be a Control Period between any two non-control periods. It uses CTL encoding to convert 2-bit data into 10-bit data.

Before each Video Data Period or Data Island Period, a Preamble is sent through the Control Period. The Preamble is a sequence of eight identical control characters used to indicate whether the upcoming data period is a Video Data Period or a Data Island Period.

The values of CTL0, CTL1, CTL2, and CTL3 indicate the type of the subsequent data period, and the remaining control signals (HSYNC and VSYNC) may also change.

  1. Data Island Period

In this phase, audio and other ancillary data are transmitted, using TERC4 encoding to convert 4-bit data into 10-bit data. The data island period consists of three parts: the front guard band, the header and data packet, and the rear guard band.

  1. Video Data Period

In this phase, actual image data is transmitted, using video data encoding to convert 8-bit data into 10-bit data. The video data period has a front guard band but no rear guard band.

Thus, D[7:0] does not transmit this data simultaneously, but rather transmits it in phases.

TMDS is a differential signal, and LVDS is also a differential signal. What is the difference between them?

  • The working principle of LVDS differential signals can be found in the LVDS interface introduction section.

  • TMDS differential signals use 2 differential lines to transmit signals, where signal 0 and 1 are determined by the voltage difference between the two pins:

    • When the TMDS differential line + is at a high level,
    • and the differential line - is at a low level, it represents 1;
    • When the differential line + is low and the differential line - is high, it represents 0.
    • The TMDS receiver determines whether the sent data is 1 or 0 by judging the voltage difference between + and -.

It also includes manufacturer information, product ID, production date, etc. As shown in the EDID structure diagram below, the 08h address occupies 2 bytes and is used to represent the manufacturer’s name, using the ISA 3-character ID code. eDP interface introduction

The full name of eDP is Embedded DisplayPort, which is an embedded display interface initiated by the Video Electronics Standards Association (VESA). The eDP protocol is an extension of the DP (Display Port) architecture and protocol for embedded applications, so the eDP protocol is fully compatible with the DP protocol. It has been widely used in laptops, tablets, and other fields.

The RK3568 supports 1 eDP interface. The features are as follows:

  2. Up to 4 physical lanes, each lane supports 2.7 Gbps

VGA interface introduction

VGA stands for Video Graphics Array, which means video image array. VGA is a protocol for video transmission using analog signals, introduced by IBM in 1987. Initially, it was very popular in desktop computer applications due to its high resolution, fast display speed, and rich colors. However, the VGA interface is relatively large in size, and as portable laptops become more and more popular, it has gradually been phased out in the field of lightweight laptops.

VGA interfaces are divided into male and female connectors. When transmitting images between the two, the VGA image transmission standard is used.
